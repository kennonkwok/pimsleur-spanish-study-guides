## Grammar Review

### New Concepts

**1. *Soy* — first-person singular of *ser***
Lesson 3 introduces *soy* (I am), the 1st-person form of *ser*, to express identity and origin.

- *Soy norteamericana.* — I'm North American.
- *Soy de Chicago.* — I'm from Chicago.
- *No soy norteamericano.* — I'm not American.

**2. *De* + city/country for origin**
To express where you are from, use *soy de* followed by the place name. *De* can also mean "of" in other contexts.

- *Soy de Los Ángeles.* — I'm from Los Angeles.
- *¿De dónde es usted?* — Where are you from? (lit. "From where are you?")

**3. Gender of nouns and article agreement**
All Spanish nouns are masculine or feminine. The definite article (*el/la*) matches the gender. This lesson highlights the contrast:

- *el día* (m.) → *buenos días*
- *la tarde* (f.) → *buenas tardes*
- *la noche* (f.) → *buenas noches*

The adjective *bueno/buena* agrees with the noun's gender.

**4. Formal self-introduction with *el/la***
When introducing yourself formally by title and name, Spanish uses the definite article before the title:

- *Soy el señor García.* — I'm Mr. García.
- *Soy la señora Jones.* — I'm Mrs. Jones.

**5. *Y usted?* — "And you?"**
To ask "and you?" in response to a question, say *¿Y usted?* The word *y* (and) can be dropped when context is clear, leaving just *¿Usted?*

### Patterns Drilled

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| State origin | *Soy de Chicago.* |
| Ask origin | *¿De dónde es usted?* |
| Greet by time of day | *Buenos días / Buenas tardes / Buenas noches* |
| Deny nationality | *No soy norteamericano.* |
| Formal introduction | *Soy el señor García. / Soy la señora Jones.* |
| Return a question | *¿Y usted?* / *¿Usted?* |
| Say good night | *Buenas noches, señora Jones.* |

### Formal vs. Informal
Lesson 3 uses only **formal** register (*usted*, titles, *señor/señora*). Informal forms are not introduced.

### Pronunciation Notes
- *De dónde*: in fast speech, the two words run together — *dedónde*. Accent on the second syllable of *dónde*.
- *Buenas tardes / noches*: the *-s* of *buenas* links to the following word in natural speech.
- *La noche y el día*: the *y* (and) in Spanish sounds like English "ee"; it does not sound like English "why."
- *Los Ángeles*: in Spanish the stress falls on *Án-* (second syllable), unlike English "Los AN-geles."

## Summary
- **entender** — stem-changing (e→ie); nosotros/vosotros keep original stem
- **estar** — irregular; used for temporary states; lesson drills *está*
- **hablar** — regular -ar
- **ser** — irregular; lesson introduces *soy* (1st person); memorize all 6 forms
