## Grammar Review

### New Concepts

**1. *Tú* vs. *usted* — informal vs. formal "you"**
Lesson 5 explicitly introduces the **informal** second-person pronoun *tú* alongside *usted*:
- *usted* — formal; used with strangers, elders, new acquaintances.
- *tú* — informal; used with friends, family, peers, children, service workers.

Verb forms differ between the two:
- *¿Tiene usted pesos?* — Do you have pesos? (formal)
- *¿Tienes tú pesos?* — Do you have pesos? (informal)
- *¿Sabe usted?* — Do you know? (formal)
- *¿Sabes tú?* — Do you know? (informal)

**2. *Tener* — "to have"**
The lesson drills three key forms of *tener*:
- *tengo* — I have (irregular *yo* form)
- *tiene* — you have (formal) / he, she has
- *tienes* — you have (informal)

**3. *Saber* — "to know" (a fact)**
Lesson 5 fully drills *saber* in context:
- *sé* — I know (irregular *yo*)
- *sabe* — you know (formal) / he, she knows
- *sabes* — you know (informal)

Pattern: *¿Sabe usted dónde está el hotel?* — Do you know where the hotel is?

**4. *Solo* + verb — "only"**
The adverb *solo* precedes the conjugated verb to mean "only":
- *Solo tengo dólares.* — I only have dollars.
- *Solo tengo un peso.* — I only have one peso.

**5. *De nada* — "You're welcome"**
Standard response to *gracias*: *De nada.* (lit. "of nothing").

**6. *Por favor* — "Please"**
Placed at the beginning or end of a request for politeness.

### Patterns Drilled

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| Ask if someone knows | *¿Sabe usted dónde está el hotel?* |
| Say you don't know | *No sé.* / *No sé dónde está.* |
| Ask if someone has pesos (formal) | *¿Tiene usted pesos?* |
| Ask if someone has pesos (informal) | *¿Tienes pesos?* |
| Say you have / don't have | *Tengo pesos.* / *No tengo pesos.* |
| Say you only have | *Solo tengo dólares.* |
| State country of origin | *Soy de Estados Unidos.* |
| Respond to thanks | *De nada.* |
| Add "please" | *Por favor.* |

### Formal vs. Informal
Lesson 5 is the **first lesson** to explicitly contrast formal (*usted / sabe / tiene*) and informal (*tú / sabes / tienes*) address. The lesson drills both registers in parallel.

### Pronunciation Notes
- *Por favor*: two words; stress on *-VOR* in the second word. Common mistake: running it into one word.
- *De nada*: the *-d-* in *nada* between vowels softens to near-silence; sounds closer to "naa" in fast speech.
- *Solo*: accent falls on first syllable: *SO-lo*.
- *Sé*: one syllable with accent to distinguish from *se* (reflexive pronoun).
- *Tienes*: two syllables: *TIE-nes*. The *ie* is a diphthong — one smooth sound.
- *Tengo*: the *ng* is as in English "long"; do not pronounce the *g* separately.

## Verb Conjugations

### tener — to have
**Type:** irregular (*yo* form: *tengo*; *tú/Ud./Uds.* are stem-changing e→ie, but those forms are not drilled until later)
For this lesson, memorize: *tengo*, *tiene*, *tienes*.

|---|---|---|

## Summary
- **entender** — stem-changing (e→ie); nosotros/vosotros keep original stem
- **estar** — irregular; used for location and temporary states
- **hablar** — regular -ar
- **saber** — irregular *yo* (*sé*); otherwise regular -er
- **ser** — irregular; memorize all 6 forms
- **tener** — irregular *yo* (*tengo*); *tú/él/Uds.* are also stem-changing (e→ie); lesson drills *tengo*, *tiene*, *tienes*
