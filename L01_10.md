## Grammar Review

### New Concepts

**1. *me gusta* — I like (it)**
*Gustar* works differently from English "like." The thing that is liked is the grammatical subject; the person who likes it is an indirect object pronoun.
- *Me gusta el refresco.* — I like soda. (Literally: The soda is pleasing to me.)
- *No me gusta el refresco.* — I don't like soda.
- *Me gusta mucho.* — I like it a lot.
- When what you like is a verb/activity: *Me gusta comer.* — I like to eat.
- When the subject is plural: *Me gustan las cervezas.* (introduced later)

**2. *¿por qué?* vs. *porque***
These two look similar but work differently:
- *¿Por qué?* (two words, accent) = Why? (question)
- *Porque* (one word, no accent) = Because (answer)
- *¿Por qué no?* — Why not?
- *Porque no me gusta.* — Because I don't like it.

**3. *setecientos* — 700 (irregular)**
Unlike other hundreds, *siete* (7) changes to *sete-* in *setecientos*:
- *cien* (100), *doscientos* (200), *trescientos* (300), *cuatrocientos* (400), *quinientos* (500, not yet drilled), *seiscientos* (600, not yet drilled), **setecientos** (700), *ochocientos* (800)
- *ocho* does NOT change: *ochocientos*

**4. *o* → *u* before *o-* sounds**
The Spanish word for "or" is normally *o*, but it changes to *u* before a word starting with the *o* sound (to avoid confusion):
- *Setecientos u ochocientos.* — Seven hundred or eight hundred.
- *Siete u ocho.* — Seven or eight.

**5. *aquí tienes* — here you have / here it is**
A common expression for handing something to someone:
- *Aquí tienes la torta.* — Here's your torta.
- Formal equivalent: *Aquí tiene usted.*

**6. *de nada* — you're welcome**
The standard response to *gracias*:
- *Muchas gracias. — De nada.* — Thank you very much. — You're welcome.
- Alternatives: *no es nada* (also introduced in prior lessons), *con mucho gusto* (not yet drilled).

### Formal vs. Informal
Lesson 10 opens with an informal exchange (*¿Cómo estás? / ¿A dónde vas?*) and continues in the *tú* register throughout the main practice. *Cómo se dice* (how do you say) is a neutral meta-phrase. The formal *¿Cómo está usted?* is reviewed in contrast.

### Pronunciation Notes
- *Agua*: *el agua* despite being feminine — takes masculine article because it starts with stressed *a*. The adjective stays feminine: *agua fría* (correct), NOT *agua frío*.
- *Setecientos*: *se-te-SYEN-tos* — *siete* loses its *i* in this compound.
- *Ochocientos*: *o-cho-SYEN-tos*.
- *Once*: *ON-se* — two syllables; the *ce* = *se* in Latin American Spanish.
- *Por qué*: two separate syllables, accent rises at end — *por-KÉ*. *Porque*: unstressed second syllable — *por-ke*.

## Verb Conjugations

### gustar — to be pleasing to (to like)
**Type:** regular -ar (but used with indirect object pronouns)
In practice, only 3rd sg. (*gusta*) and 3rd pl. (*gustan*) appear; the conjugation below is the full present indicative.

| | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | yo: gusto | nosotros: gustamos |
| 2nd | tú: gustas | vosotros: gustáis |
| 3rd | él/ella/Ud.: **gusta** | ellos/ellas/Uds.: **gustan** |

Lesson 10 drills: *Me gusta el refresco.* / *No me gusta el refresco.* / *Me gusta comer.* (all 3rd sg. only).

## Summary
- **gustar** — regular -ar but used with indirect object pronouns; *me gusta* = I like it
- **ir** — irregular; yo: *voy*, tú: *vas*, nosotros: *vamos*
- **estar** — irregular; *¿cómo estás?* = how are you? (informal)
- **querer** — stem-changing (e→ie); yo: *quiero*
- **tener** — irregular (go-verb); yo: *tengo*; *aquí tienes* = here you have
- **comer** — regular -er
- **tomar** — regular -ar; to drink (beverages)
- **saber** — irregular; yo: *sé*
- **pagar** — regular -ar; used with *ir a* + infinitive construction
