## Grammar Review

### New Concepts

**1. Numbers 32–35 and 300–400**
Spanish compound numbers (21–29) fuse into one word (*treinta y dos*). Hundreds follow a pattern:
- *doscientos* (200), *trescientos* (300), *cuatrocientos* (400)
- Note: *cien* is used alone for 100; *ciento* in compounds (*ciento veinte* = 120)
- Hundreds agree in gender: *cuatrocientas personas* (f.)

**2. *Vamos a* + infinitive ("let's...")**
*Vamos a comer* = let's go eat (literally: we go to eat). This construction uses the present indicative of *ir* (1st person plural) + *a* + infinitive. It is the standard way to express "let's + verb":
- *Vamos a comer.* — Let's go eat.
- *Vamos a comer en el hotel.* — Let's go eat in the hotel.

**3. *en* vs. *a* (in vs. to)**
- *en el hotel* = in the hotel (location)
- *al hotel* = to the hotel (direction; *a + el* → *al*)

**4. Formal vs. informal — switching registers**
Lesson 7 explicitly contrasts *entiende muy bien* (formal, Ud.) with the informal *¿entiendes bien?* (tú). This is the first lesson to prompt the learner to switch registers consciously.

**5. *saber* — to know (a fact or how to do something)**
*Saber* is used for knowing facts or information, not for knowing people (*conocer*). The lesson introduces:
- *¿Sabes dónde está el hotel?* — Do you know where the hotel is? (informal)
- *Yo sé dónde está.* — I know where it is.

### Patterns Drilled

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| State how many pesos you have | *Solo tengo treinta y dos pesos.* |
| Say you have pesos elsewhere | *Pero tengo pesos en el hotel.* |
| Ask if someone knows where something is (informal) | *¿Sabes dónde está el hotel?* |
| State your nationality/origin | *Soy de Estados Unidos.* |
| Ask someone's origin (formal) | *¿Es usted de México?* |
| State you speak/don't speak a language | *Hablo español. / No hablo mucho inglés.* |
| Let's go + infinitive | *Vamos a comer.* |
| Express wanting to eat something | *Quiero comer algo.* |
| Negate with *nada* | *No quiero nada. / No quiero comer nada.* |

### Formal vs. Informal
Both registers appear this lesson. Formal (*usted*): *¿Habla inglés? / Entiende muy bien.* Informal (*tú*): *¿Entiendes? / ¿Sabes dónde está?* Lesson explicitly asks learner to produce the informal equivalent of formal utterances.

### Pronunciation Notes
- *Treinta*: the *ei* diphthong sounds like "ay" in "day" — *TREYN-tah*.
- *Trescientos*: stress falls on *cien* — *tres-SYEN-tos*.
- *Estados Unidos*: four syllables in *Es-TA-dos u-NI-dos*; the *d* between vowels is soft, close to English "th."
- *Paraguay*: stress on the final syllable — *pa-ra-GWAI*.

## Summary
- **tener** — irregular (go-verb); yo: *tengo*
- **saber** — irregular; yo: *sé*
- **estar** — irregular (go-verb); yo: *estoy*; used for location and temporary states
- **ser** — irregular; used for identity, origin, and permanent characteristics
- **ir** — irregular; *vamos* = let's go / we go; *vamos a* + infinitive = let's + verb
- **querer** — stem-changing (e→ie); yo: *quiero*
- **hablar** — regular -ar
- **entender** — stem-changing (e→ie)
- **comer** — regular -er
