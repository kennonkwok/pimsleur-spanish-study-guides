## Grammar Review

### New Concepts

**1. Future tense with *ser*: *será***
The simple future of *ser* is fully irregular. In this lesson it appears repeatedly in the fixed phrase *será divertido / será interesante*.

- *Será divertido.* — It will be fun.
- *Será muy interesante.* — It will be very interesting.

**2. *Ojalá (que)* + present subjunctive**
*Ojalá* expresses hope or a wish. It always triggers the subjunctive. The connector *que* is optional.

- *Ojalá que no llueva.* — Hopefully it doesn't rain. (*llueva* = present subjunctive of *llover*)
- The subjunctive stem for *llover*: *llover* → *lluev-* → *llueva*

**3. *Quizás* + present subjunctive**
*Quizás* (maybe/perhaps) can take either indicative (higher certainty) or subjunctive (lower certainty). This lesson drills the subjunctive:

- *Quizás mañana vayamos a la playa.* — Maybe tomorrow we'll go to the beach.
- *vayamos* = present subjunctive nosotros of *ir* (fully irregular: *vaya, vayas, vaya, vayamos, vayáis, vayan*)

**4. Conditional of *gustar*: *gustaría***
*gustaría* is the conditional (would like) of *gustar*. It follows the same reverse construction as the present:

- *Le gustaría ir con nosotros.* — Would you like to go with us?
- *Sí, me gustaría.* — Yes, I'd like to.

**5. Imperfect tense (*tenía*, *tenía*)**
The lesson introduces the imperfect of *tener* to describe what one used to have or had (over an extended / indefinite period):

- *¿Qué tenía en la billetera?* — What did you have in your wallet?
- *Tenía un poco de dinero.* — I had a little money.
- Contrasts with preterite (*tuve*) which would mark a specific completed event.

**6. Preterite of *ir* / *ser*: *fue*, *fuimos***
*Ir* and *ser* share the same preterite forms (context disambiguates):

- *El sábado fue muy estresante.* — Saturday was very stressful. (ser)
- *Mi sobrino y yo fuimos a un partido.* — My nephew and I went to a game. (ir)

**7. *por si acaso* vs. *en todo caso***
Two distinct expressions involving *caso*:

| Expression | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| *por si acaso* | just in case | *Lo traje por si acaso.* |
| *en todo caso* | in any case / at any rate | *En todo caso, será divertido.* |

**8. Post-noun adjective stacking with *y***
When two descriptive adjectives of equal weight follow a noun, they are joined with *y*:

- *mi paraguas grande y rojo* — my big and red umbrella
- Word order: noun → adj1 + *y* + adj2

**9. *manejar* vs. *conducir* (regional variation)**
Both mean "to drive." The lesson explicitly teaches both and notes regional usage:

- *manejar* — Mexico and most of Latin America
- *conducir* — Spain and much of South America
- Driver's license: *licencia de manejar* (MX) / *licencia de conducir* (ES/SA)

## Summary

- **ser** — highly irregular; lesson focuses on future (*será*) for predictions and preterite (*fue*, *fuimos*) for past events
- **ir** — irregular; lesson focuses on present subjunctive (*vayamos*) after *quizás* and preterite (*fuimos*) shared with *ser*
- **tener** — irregular; lesson introduces imperfect (*tenía*) for describing what one habitually had or had over a period
- **llover** — stem-changing (o→ue), impersonal; *llueva* = present subjunctive drilled with *ojalá que*
- **comer** — regular -er; lesson drills simple future (*comeremos*)
- **traer** — irregular; lesson drills preterite yo (*traje*) in *lo traje por si acaso*
- **dejar** — regular -ar; lesson drills preterite yo (*dejé*) in *afortunadamente dejé mis tarjetas en casa*
- **jugar** — stem-changing (u→ue); drilled in 3rd person singular present for match/team contexts
- **manejar** — regular -ar; Mexican usage for "to drive"; introduced via *licencia de manejar*
- **conducir** — irregular; Spain/SA usage for "to drive"; introduced via *licencia de conducir*
